Measles is back — a trust problem, not a supply problem

By Md Shafaat Ali Choyon · builds & runs AI in production · Growth & health strategist · 5 min read

A measles outbreak in 2026 America is not a story about a missing vaccine. The vaccine has existed, cheaply, for sixty years. It's a story about trust — and it's one I've watched play out on the other side of the world.

The numbers that should stop us

By early July 2026 the US had confirmed more than 2,200 measles cases, on track to blow past the 2025 total. The reason isn't supply. It's that kindergarten MMR coverage has slipped to about 92.5%, under the roughly 95% needed for herd immunity, and the overwhelming majority of cases are in the unvaccinated. When enough people opt out, a disease we had nearly eliminated comes back.

Facts don't change the hesitant

Here's what sixteen years in health communication taught me: you don't close a trust gap with more facts. A hesitant parent has usually already heard the facts. What moves them is who is saying it — a person they already trust, in a place they already are. Treat hesitancy as an information deficit and you lose. Treat it as a relationship problem and you have a chance.

What Bangladesh already figured out

Bangladesh — poorer, denser, far harder to reach — runs one of the most respected immunization programs in the world, with coverage sustained well above 90% for years. How? Not slicker ads. An army of community health workers who live in the neighborhoods they serve, backed by mothers' groups, local leaders, and relentless door-to-door reach. Trust gets built face to face first; vaccination follows.

The US doesn't have a vaccine problem. It has a trusted-messenger problem — and that one is designable.

The fix is a communication strategy

Rebuilding coverage looks less like a new drug and more like a campaign: segment the hesitant, meet them on the channels they actually use, recruit messengers they respect, and drop the shaming tone that only hardens opposition. That isn't soft work around the edges of public health. It is the intervention.

The short version

  • Measles is surging because coverage fell below herd immunity — a trust failure, not a supply one.
  • More facts don't move hesitancy; trusted messengers do.
  • Bangladesh sustains 90%+ coverage through community health workers, not better advertising.
  • The US fix is a segmentation-and-messenger strategy, delivered without shame.

If you were rebuilding vaccine confidence in your own community, who is the one messenger people would actually believe — and are we resourcing them?
