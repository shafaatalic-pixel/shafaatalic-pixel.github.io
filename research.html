Research interests · seven questions my career handed me

1 · When do consumers trust AI-generated content and advice?

AI now drafts the messages, recommendations and guidance consumers act on. Under what conditions do people accept, discount, or defer to AI-mediated content — and how do design commitments (disclosure, provenance, human oversight, "never-fabricate" guardrails) change trust, persuasion, and downstream behavior? Healthcare is the sharpest application, but the question spans every category.

Where this comes from: I build and operate agentic AI production systems with human-in-the-loop QA — including a full-stack AI application governed by a strict never-fabricate guardrail. Operating these systems daily raised the question I now want to test: which guardrails do consumers actually perceive, and which ones change acceptance?

2 · What substitutes for trust in low-trust markets?

When institutional trust is absent — used-goods markets, informal economies — what signals can firms engineer to make strangers transact? Which trust surrogates (verification rituals, physical presence, third-party inspection) work, and do they build durable market trust or only borrow it?

Where this comes from: At Ekhanei.com I built Bangladesh's #1 motorbike marketplace category in 2.5 months by engineering trust: government registration checks on site, live audit-call quality loops, a physical "Bike Bazaar" fair where ~30% of inventory sold in a day and users grew ~50%. The intervention worked; the theory of why is a dissertation.

3 · How do premium products win in value-driven markets?

When local demand clusters at mid-range price points, what makes consumers cross the premium threshold? How do affordability architecture (installments, trade-ins), engineered scarcity, and price-quality signaling interact — and when does scarcity build a brand rather than merely ration it?

Where this comes from: At Samsung I ran the Galaxy Note9 launch in a market dominated by Tk 30–50k handsets — multi-bank 0% EMI, channel expansion, deliberate stock-out scarcity — producing 1.54× YoY preorder growth and the #1 global ranking for launch growth; and the J2 line at ~2,600 units/day via psychological price points and retailer trust. Two natural experiments in pricing psychology.

4 · How should financial products be marketed to first-time, low-literacy users?

Financial inclusion fails at the marketing layer as often as the product layer. What message channels and trust anchors (community institutions, voice-first interfaces, human intermediaries) move first-time users from suspicion to adoption of digital money — and which anchors scale?

Where this comes from: At SureCash I ran brand, trade and PR for a 10-million-beneficiary government-to-person stipend rollout on a multi-bank fintech platform — rural mosque activations, automated IVR in local dialect, retailer mapping against beneficiary residence data. Population-scale field evidence on trust-anchored adoption.

5 · Can ethical pricing be a growth strategy in credence goods?

Healthcare is the classic credence good — consumers can't evaluate quality, so intermediaries (and kickbacks) fill the gap. Does removing hidden incentives and pricing transparently grow demand in such markets, and how do consumers infer integrity from price architecture?

Where this comes from: At Praava Health we replaced the customary 40–50% physician-kickback model with transparent, organ-targeted diagnostic packages — and lab margin rose from ~30% to ~50% while revenue grew. Ethics outperformed the kickback economy; I want to understand the consumer psychology that made it work.

6 · Can everyday products be gateways to services?

In markets where service-seeking is rare and trust in institutions low, can mass-market FMCG channels carry services — healthcare, finance, information — to populations that institutions can't reach? What does the daily-product context do to the perceived credibility of the offer?

Where this comes from: The Praava × Lifebuoy partnership printed a health hotline on soap — a daily-product gateway that reached roughly 3.6 million people (~2% of Bangladesh's population) during the pandemic, feeding teleconsultation, triage and district-level illness mapping shared with the national health directorate.

7 · How should messages be framed for audiences that resist them?

Elimination messaging backfires with audiences whose culture normalizes the behavior. When does harm-reduction framing outperform abstinence framing, and how should one body of evidence be re-framed across audiences without losing integrity?

Where this comes from: I designed a year-long, four-channel campus harm-reduction campaign grounded in health-behavior theory (non-stigmatizing framing, audience segmentation, built-in evaluation), and my MPH capstone documented ~3× reach amplification through strategic message design. This site's own audience-adaptive architecture is the same idea, applied.
